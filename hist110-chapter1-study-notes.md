# Chapter 1 Study Notes — Age of Exploration and the Slave Trade

---

## Treaty of Tordesillas (1494)

- Signed by Spain and Portugal to divide Atlantic territories
- Line drawn 370 leagues west of Cape Verde Islands
- Portugal got everything east of the line (including Brazil and the African coast)
- Spain got everything west of the line (the Americas)
- Created a framework for European colonial domination
- No recognition of indigenous sovereignty
- Brazil's placement confirmed it as Portuguese territory

---

## King Nzinga Mbemba Letter (1526)

- Written 32 years after Tordesillas
- King of Kongo converts to Catholicism, welcomes Portuguese missionaries
- Original arrangement: war captives traded for Portuguese arms
- Problem: Portuguese merchants started grabbing free Kongolese citizens directly
- Mbemba's complaint was about the loss of royal control, not slavery itself
- Three effects he describes: depopulation, collapse of royal authority, moral corruption
- His kingdom was being depopulated at an accelerating rate
- Subjects stopped obeying because Portuguese goods were more abundant than what Mbemba could offer

---

## The Slave Trade Machine

- Treaty of Tordesillas created the structure; Mbemba's letter shows it was already running by 1526
- Portugal controlled the supply (African coast)
- Spain controlled the demand (American colonies)
- The system was deliberate, contractual, and growing
- African kingdoms like Kongo were caught between being Christian allies and victims of the trade

---

## The Armada Portrait (1588)

- Painted to celebrate England's defeat of the Spanish Armada
- Protestant England vs. Catholic Spain
- Elizabeth I depicted with:
  - Hand on globe, fingers on North America — global ambition, challenge to Iberian monopoly
  - Imperial State Crown — legitimate royal authority
  - Two background panels: calm English fleet vs. wrecked Spanish Armada
- Portrait function: propaganda, legitimacy, divine favor narrative

---

## Demographic Impact (Glossary Note)

- European and Asian populations soared from American crops (maize, potatoes)
- African population remained stagnant despite receiving the same crops
- The transatlantic slave trade created a demographic drain that stunted Africa's growth for centuries

---

## Key Vocabulary

- **Roman Catholicism:** Christian practices of the Roman Catholic Church; exerted political, economic, and social influence across western Europe and the Americas through Spanish and Portuguese empires
- **Protestant Reformation:** 16th-century religious movement initiated by Martin Luther; teaching that Christians can communicate directly with God, challenging Catholic Church authority and corruption
- **Reconquista:** Spain's centuries-long campaign to retake the Iberian Peninsula from Muslim rule; completed in 1492 with the fall of Granada
- **Transatlantic Slave Trade:** System of forcibly transporting enslaved Africans to the Americas; grew rapidly after 1494 as American colonies demanded labor for mines and plantations

---

## Key Takeaways

1. European exploration was not random — it was organized, competitive, and contractual (Tordesillas)
2. The slave trade was built into the system from the beginning, not an accident
3. Africa was not passive — kingdoms like Kongo resisted, negotiated, and adapted
4. England entered the global power contest later but aggressively, challenging Iberian dominance
5. The human cost of this system was visible and documented within a single generation
