Instructions Assignment

Write a set of instructions for performing a task you know very well--by heart--for readers who never have performed this task before. Your readers should have a compelling need for these instructions. You know that your topic is appropriate for the assignment if you don’t need to look at any other instructions to do the task. Do NOT consult any other printed/online instructions to do this assignment; you must be an expert on the task and put only your own knowledge and work into the instructions.

Remember, this assignment is to write instructions (specific, detailed actions), NOT procedures (general guidelines). The instructions may involve a device: assembling it, operating it, or fixing it. The topic might involve a process, such as for completing a certain task at work or in a lab. You may choose the task from your major, a hobby, an organization/club, your home life, or a current or past job. The task needs to happen the same way every time, with the same steps in the same order (thus, playing a game is NOT an appropriate choice). Importantly, you must be able to write the instructions from your own experience and perspective without reading instructions on the task written by others. The final document will be only 4-5 pages long,
including visual aids. Keep in mind the following limits when choosing the task:

• Choose a task at a moderate level of difficulty. Some tasks are too easy to need detailed instructions or would be taught best verbally. Other tasks are so complex that they would require a book-length manual. You want something appropriately covered in 4-5 pages.

• Make sure that the device or process has discrete steps and parts that are fairly easy to name and refer to. Do NOT use non-componential tasks, such as driving a stick shift car.

• Make sure that the task takes at least 15 minutes and no more than 60 minutes to perform (for someone new to the task).

• Make sure that the task happens essentially the same way every time; it can be replicated (there is little to no variation in what happens; there are predictable results). Note: Due to this requirement, teaching a game is not an appropriate choice.

• Ideally, have the materials or equipment needed to carry out the task at hand or accessible during the writing process, so you can review the task and recall important details to share.

• Avoid tasks that are expensive or dangerous to perform. Follow the guidelines for instructions described in Ch. 10 of our textbook. The steps should represent a logical division of actions and be clearly expressed as imperatives (with a command action verb leading each step) and results, with any necessary explanations. Number each main action step. Help the reader assess whether
or not the task (or a step in it) was completed successfully, and tell the reader how to solve common problems (troubleshoot). Be sure to give any warnings or cautions necessary for safety of user and equipment. Include any notes helpful for checking progress or confirming results throughout the process. 

Instructions communicate visually as well as verbally (review Ch. 6 about visual aids). Your instructions must include at least one visual aid (such as screen shots or photos), but you are likely to need more. Some instructions have a visual aid for each step, and some have visual aids for selected key steps where they are most needed. Most students create their own visuals, but if you truly need to use a visual aid created by someone else, you must acknowledge the source to avoid plagiarism (for this assignment, you can do this with a brief caption beneath the visual aid). In addition, your instructions must use an effective page design that makes the information accessible (review Ch. 5 about page design). Remember to consider the conditions under which
the document would be used when designing it.
