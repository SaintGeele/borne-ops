Instructions for Writing a Topic Memo

Purpose and Grading of Topic Memos:
As you approach each new major writing assignment (project) in this course, you first will write a one-page Topic Memo to me. This memo lets me know what you plan to write about and how you will
complete the task. I want you to show me that you have thought carefully about the assignment and
have a solid plan.

Of course, writing each Topic Memo to me also gives you practice writing memos. You must use
correct memo format, as shown in the textbook (with To, From, Date, and Subject headings in the
upper left-hand corner), addressed to Dr. Jirousek-Falls, with a brief and descriptive phrase as
memo subject line. Do not use the phrase “Topic Memo” as your subject line; rather use something
specific like “Letter to Verizon” or “Report on AI in Medicine”—something that really forecasts what you plan to write about. Also use the 4 sections with headings detailed on the next page of these instructions.

Topic Memos submitted on time may earn up to 10 points.

When I grade your memo, I will give you any needed feedback to apply when writing the full
assignment and perhaps ask for slight adjustments in your approach.

Very rarely, I may need to ask you to submit a new Topic Memo on a new topic because the first one
just isn’t going to work for the assignment (such as, if you did not follow the assignment instructions or did not include enough information); in this case, you need to submit the new memo as soon as possible, preferably within a day after I grade the first one, in order to stay on track for completing the project and in order to earn substantial points for the Topic Memo. If you submitted a Topic Memo on time and see a zero grade, you will know immediately that I didn’t approve the topic, and you will see why when you read my grading comments; if you submit a new Topic Memo in a timely manner, you may still earn points (replacing the zero grade) for the Topic Memo.

There are other times when you submit a Topic Memo, and in my grading comments, I let you know
that the topic overall is fine but that you need to make a few adjustments to your plan as you proceed with the main assignment; you may or may not have earned all the memo points in these cases, depending on the quality of what you submitted. Sometimes, students have a viable topic but are not very detailed or don’t follow Topic Memo format guidelines and have the topic approved but don’t earn full points for the memo; the comments tell them what to do better for the NEXT project’s Topic Memo to earn full points. Therefore, it’s extremely important first that you READ, and then that you APPLY all of my feedback comments on Topic Memos. If I have given you points on a Topic Memo, do NOT redo it. Just apply the feedback on your upcoming main project and possibly to future Topic memos.

If you submit an initial Topic Memo within 24 hours late, you may earn up to 9 points. I want you to follow instructions carefully and submit on time. Class moves too fast to delay Topic Memos. Topic Memos submitted after that will earn fewer points, but still are required, in order to submit the related, upcoming project. The Topic Memo stays open for a few days more than other assignments, but then closes completely for further submissions. You will see the closing date in Canvas. If you want to do the upcoming project, submit a Topic Memo for it before that closing date.

When I grade the entire set of Topic Memos, if you haven’t submitted, I will put in a zero. That zero should remind/prompt you to submit a Topic Memo as soon as possible. I will not otherwise remind you. If you haven’t had your topic approved first, I will not accept your main assignment for a grade.

Required Format for Topic Memos:
Use the four (4) headings below to organize your memo body content (but don’t forget the To: From:
Date: and Subject: lines at the top left-hand corner of the page first). The accompanying questions are guidelines on the kinds of things to cover for each of the four content areas (you don’t necessarily have to respond directly to all of them). Write one brief paragraph of complete sentences under each heading. Single-space your text. Include a line space before each new heading. Make each heading stand out from regular text, such as by using bold print (as you see below) or underlining. The body of your memo will be formatted as you see below (but you will have informative sentences, not questions).

Topic
What specifically are you going to write about? Is it a problem? an idea? a procedure? What is it made up of? What are the "parts" of your topic? What questions/issues/problems are involved?
What makes your topic worth writing about?

Audience
To whom specifically will you be writing? Who should care? Who needs to read? Remember your
audience is not the professor or the class; we want to include another name or at least a role/job title of the reader(s) here. What is your reader like? What do they know, think, or expect about you and your topic? Is their thinking significantly different from yours? Will they be hostile to what you have to say? What is the best way to write to them? Will you need to convince them that your topic is significant, or do they already know this?

Purpose
What effect or impact do you want to have on your audience, such as to create interest or persuade them to take a certain action? How will it be possible for you to have this effect or make this impact?

Strategy
What will you need to do in order to prepare for writing? What kinds of research or investigation will you need to do? What kinds of writing strategies are you likely to use?
What kinds of details or subtopics do you plan to cover? How do you plan to organize your material?
Your whole Topic Memo should fit on one page.
