# Chapter 1: Comparing Perspectives — Boundaries in the Age of Exploration

## PRIMARY SOURCE 1: Treaty of Tordesillas, June 7, 1494

**Historical Context**

The Treaty of Tordesillas was negotiated in 1494, just two years after Columbus's 1522 voyage and Spain's completion of the Reconquista with the fall of Granada. Spain and Portugal were locked in intense competition over newly discovered territories in the Atlantic. Portugal had already established claims along Africa's coast through earlier treaties, and the 1493 papal bull *Inter caetera* had granted Spain rights to lands west of the Cape Verde Islands, which Portugal challenged. This treaty was the resolution: a negotiated boundary dividing the Atlantic 370 leagues west of the Cape Verde Islands, designed to prevent war and formalize each crown's sphere of influence.

**Perspective**

The treaty's perspective is entirely that of European Catholic monarchies operating from the assumption that they had the authority to divide non-Christian lands between themselves. There is no acknowledgment of any indigenous sovereignty, no recognition of the peoples who already inhabited the territories being allocated. The language treats these lands as essentially unclaimed despite the obvious presence of established civilizations in Africa, the Americas, and Asia. The document reveals a worldview in which Christian European crowns held dominion over the globe's unclaimed or "heathen" territories by right of discovery and papal precedent.

**Purpose**

The stated purpose is explicit: for the sake of peace and concord, and for the preservation of the relationship and love of the said King of Portugal for the said King and Queen of Castile. The treaty was designed to legally resolve the territorial dispute between Spain and Portugal and prevent armed conflict over New World claims. It functioned as a colonizer's framework, a contractual agreement between two powers to share the world in a way that served their commercial and political interests, dressed in the language of Christian legitimacy and mutual respect between crowns.

## PRIMARY SOURCE 2: Letter from King Nzinga Mbemba of the Kongo, to King João of Portugal, July 6, 1526

**Historical Context**

By 1526, Portugal and the Kingdom of Kongo had an established relationship dating back to the late 1400s, when Portuguese missionaries had converted the Kongolese king and his court to Catholicism. Early exchanges were relatively balanced, involving trade in goods like ivory and copper. However, as the transatlantic slave trade accelerated in the early 1500s, Portuguese merchants increasingly turned to the Kongo as a source of enslaved people. Originally, the arrangement had been war captives exchanged for Portuguese arms. By the mid-1520s, Portuguese merchants had begun bypassing the royal monopoly entirely, grabbing free Kongolese citizens including nobles and royal relatives. By the mid-1520s, the scale of the trade had reached a breaking point.

**Perspective**

King Nzinga Mbemba writes from the perspective of a sovereign watching his kingdom disintegrate. His tone blends urgency with moral authority, invoking both God's will and the contractual nature of the Portugal-Kongo relationship to condemn the slave trade. Critically, he positions himself as a victim of Portuguese-backed exploitation. Merchants operating under the guise of legitimate commerce were effectively depopulating his realm. He frames his complaint not as anti-Portuguese, but as pro-Christian and pro-order, essentially calling on Portugal to live up to the very Christian values both kingdoms shared. His perspective is also deeply personal. He describes his own nobles and relatives being taken, placing himself within the same social fabric being torn apart.

**Purpose**

Mbemba's stated purpose is explicit in the opening line. Our Kingdom is being lost in so many ways that it is convenient to provide for the necessary remedy. He wants the slave trade halted immediately. The underlying purpose is to restore the authority of the Kongo crown. His own subjects no longer obey him because Portuguese merchants have made them content with goods they can no longer obtain without participating in the trade. His purpose is also to hold Portugal accountable to the Christian moral framework both kingdoms shared. There is an implicit threat in the letter. If Portugal does not act, the kingdom and by extension the Christian alliance will collapse.

---

## QUESTION 1

The 370-league line gave Portugal control of everything east of it, which locked in their grip on the African coast, the same coast that would become the primary source of enslaved people for the entire Atlantic trade. Spain got the Americas, but those lands needed labor to be worth anything, and there was no way to build mines or plantations without massive amounts of human beings. The treaty essentially split the work. Portugal supplied the people. Spain consumed them. By the 1520s, that machine was already running at full speed.

---

## QUESTION 2

Mbemba lays out three disasters at once. The country is being emptied. Merchants are grabbing free people off the street, including his own relatives and nobles, and selling them. His own authority is crumbling because subjects can get Portuguese goods easier than they can get what he offers them, so they stopped listening. And the moral decay is everywhere, driven by traders he calls thieves acting without conscience. The kingdom he built is being eaten alive by the very people he thought he was partnering with.

---

## QUESTION 3

These documents show the slave trade was not some accident of exploration. It was a system. The Treaty of Tordesillas gave Spain and Portugal a written agreement to carve up the world's resources, and Mbemba's letter proves that system was already destroying lives just three decades later. His desperation tells us the demand for labor in the Americas was so enormous that Portuguese merchants were willing to tear apart an allied Christian kingdom to fill it. Europe built the machine, drew up the contracts, and lit the fuse. The Kingdom of Kongo felt the blast first.

## VISUAL EXPLORATIONS: The Armada Portrait

**Historical Context**

Painted around 1588 to commemorate England's defeat of the Spanish Armada, this portrait celebrates Protestant England's victory over Catholic Spain during a period of intense religious conflict following the Protestant Reformation. The Armada was Spain's attempt to invade England and overthrow Queen Elizabeth I, and its failure marked a turning point in European power dynamics.

**Perspective**

This is English royal propaganda. The portrait presents Elizabeth I not just as a monarch, but as a powerful, divine-appointed ruler presiding over a triumphant England. The painting is designed to project strength, legitimacy, and naval dominance at a moment when England was asserting itself as a global power, competing directly with Spain and Portugal's earlier divisions of the world.

**Purpose**

The purpose is to immortalize and amplify England's victory, to reinforce Elizabeth's authority as a Protestant queen who protected her nation, and to send a message both domestically and internationally that England was a force to be reckoned with. The two background panels showing the battle and the storm-wrecked Spanish fleet function as a narrative of triumph and divine favor.

**Symbolism**

The globe under her hand. Elizabeth's fingers rest on the eastern coast of North America, signaling England's emerging global ambitions and claim to overseas territories. This is a direct challenge to the Spanish and Portuguese dominance established by the Treaty of Tordesillas just a century earlier.

The Imperial State Crown beside her asserts her legitimate authority as queen, separate from the Pope or any foreign Catholic power.

The Armada being wrecked in the right panel shows divine retribution against Catholic Spain, framing England's victory as providential rather than merely military.

Her elaborate dress with pearls and bows displays the wealth and stability of her reign despite the chaos shown in the background panels.

The calm English fleet on the left versus the stormy Spanish defeat on the right creates a visual contrast between English order and Spanish chaos, reinforcing the narrative of Protestant civilization triumphing over Catholic tyranny.
