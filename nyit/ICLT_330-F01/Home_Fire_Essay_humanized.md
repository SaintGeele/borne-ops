# The ache of what's missing: Isma's search for home through touch in *Home Fire*

Most people think of home as a place. An address. A city you can point to on a map. That makes sense on the surface, and I probably would have said the same thing before reading Kamila Shamsie's *Home Fire*. What Shamsie does in Chapter 1, though, is tear that assumption apart. Through her protagonist Isma, she makes the case that home is not a location at all. It is a physical experience, built on touch, on the feeling of someone you love being close enough to reach. And when displacement strips that away, no amount of technology can fill the gap.

Isma's understanding of home is rooted in a specific kind of contact. After losing both their mother and grandmother, Isma stepped into the role of raising her twin siblings, Aneeka and Parvaiz. She was young. She carried that weight anyway. In that household, touch became the language they used to take care of each other. Shamsie captures it in one small moment: "it was Aneeka who would place her hands on her sister's shoulders and massage away the ache" (Shamsie 18). Hands on shoulders. Pressure against pain. That is what home felt like for Isma. Not London as a city, not four walls and a roof. It was the physical reality of a sister who could feel your tension and do something about it. That word "place" is no accident either; it means intentional, careful, not a random slap or rest. "Massage away the ache" covers both the literal fatigue and the heavy emotional burden Isma carried as the eldest. Aneeka was more than just a sister here; she was a caretaker, reversing that dynamic, and that mutual care was the very heartbeat of their home. This quote gives us the baseline, the "before" picture of home: a human touch, unremarkable in its presence, utterly devastating once gone. So when Isma leaves for America, she does not just lose a zip code. She loses the one thing that made anywhere feel like home.

Even once she arrives in Massachusetts, Isma reaches for physical connection almost on instinct. At Logan Airport, she encounters snow for the first time. She does not just look at it. She touches it: "She walked over to a mound of snow at the edge of the parking lot, took off her gloves, and pressed her fingertips down on it. At first it resisted, but then it gave way, and her fingers burrowed into the softer layers beneath" (Shamsie 4). There is something telling in this moment. Isma is testing this new place through her body, almost asking whether it will accept her. That hesitant "pressed her fingertips down" shows a tentative exploration, like reading braille for a place she does not yet understand. And the answer is cold. The snow resists before it yields. Massachusetts does not pull her in. It tolerates her. "Resisted" and "gave way" perfectly map onto the immigrant experience: initial rejection, then a grudging, conditional acceptance. "Burrowed" is raw, animal instinct, seeking shelter. But burrowing into snow is paradoxical. You are digging into the thing that is cold, dangerous. Compared to Aneeka's hands working the knots out of her shoulders, the difference is everything. One is warmth and care. The other is a cold transaction, a deeply lonely substitute for human contact.

The hardest part of the chapter comes when Isma confronts what technology actually cannot do. She sees Parvaiz's name pop up on Skype, a reminder that her brother has drifted somewhere unreachable. And then she thinks about Aneeka, and what video calls have taken from them: “Touch” was the one thing modern technology didn't allow, and without it she and her sister had lost something vital to their way of being together" (Shamsie 18). That line hits because it is so plain and true. The "one thing" makes it absolute; there is no workaround, no tech solution. "Vital" means essential, life itself. Without touch, something in their relationship is literally dying. It was "their way of being together," not just "being together," highlighting a specific, almost sacred mode of connection that technology just broke. Shamsie breaks down the illusion that screens can create real closeness. You can see a face. You can hear a voice. You cannot feel a hand on your shoulder. Shamsie does not overdramatize this. She just states it, and lets the weight of it land.

Then comes the moment that stuck with me the most. Alone in her apartment, Isma reaches for her own shoulder: "She touched her shoulder, muscles knotted beneath the skin. Pressed down, and knew what it was to be without family; no one's hands but your own to minister your own suffering" (Shamsie 18). I have been far from the people I care about. That gesture, trying to comfort yourself because nobody else is there to do it, is one of the loneliest things a person can do. This is Quotes 1 and 3 colliding. She knows what home feels like (Aneeka's hands), she knows technology fails (the screen problem), and now she is living the consequence: self administered care that only highlights the absence. The "muscles knotted beneath the skin" shows her tension, both physical and metaphorical; she is carrying everything alone now. That word "minister" is the killer. It is religious, pastoral—to minister is to serve, to tend to suffering as a sacred act. Shamsie is telling us what Aneeka did for Isma was ministry. And ministry needs another person. You cannot perform that sacred act for yourself. Isma is performing the same act Aneeka used to perform for her. The motion is identical. The meaning is completely different. That is not just loneliness. That is home collapsing in on itself.

Shamsie's exploration in Chapter 1 establishes a definition of home entirely detached from geography and lays bare the brutal insufficiency of its substitutes. For Isma, home was visceral and tactile: her sister's palms pressing into sore muscles, bodies intimately sharing space and radiating warmth. America offered safety and the architecture of a better future, but it could never deliver belonging. No video call bridges that gap. Home, for Isma, resides irrevocably in the body and in the specific individuals who inhabit her life; without them, she remains rootless on any soil in the world. This is exactly the kind of corporeal loss that pushes the analysis past literature and into the territory of how technology actually functions in displaced lives. If touch really is the irreducible core of home, then the question is not whether haptic feedback systems can simulate it. The question is whether technology itself has been quietly widening the gap between people and the intimacy they are trying to hold onto. Consider what communication used to demand. A handwritten letter required time, intention, and a kind of faith. The person writing it sat with their thoughts long enough to commit them to paper. They folded it, sent it, and then waited, with no guarantee it would arrive, no read receipt, no delivery confirmation. That uncertainty was part of the weight. It meant something precisely because it cost something. Now a message crosses the world in seconds, and somehow the people on either end feel further apart than ever. Shamsie's novel does not resolve that paradox, and it should not have to. What it does is more foundational. It frames the stakes with total clarity, showing that the body remembers what screens cannot transmit and that easier access to communication has never been the same thing as closeness. If home really comes from the deliberate and effortful act of choosing someone over and over again, not just from being near them, then the very tools we built to keep us closer might be the ones quietly robbing us of what home actually feels like.
