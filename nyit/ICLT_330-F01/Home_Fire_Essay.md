# The Weight of Absence: Touch and the Search for Home in Home Fire

In discussions of displacement, one might assume that home is defined by location—a physical place one can return to. However, in Kamila Shamsie's *Home Fire*, the protagonist Isma reveals that home is not a place at all, but a bodily experience defined by touch. In Chapter 1, Shamsie uses touch to show that Isma's sense of home rests on physical closeness with her siblings—something that technology cannot replace and that displacement has painfully stripped away.

Isma's clearest memory of home is rooted in physical touch. After their mother and grandmother died, Isma raised her twin siblings, Aneeka and Parvaiz. Through the weight of that responsibility, touch became her family's language of care. As Isma reflects, "it was Aneeka who would place her hands on her sister's shoulders and massage away the ache" (Shamsie 18). This single gesture—hands on shoulders, massaging away pain—encapsulates what home meant to Isma. It was not a house or a city, but the physical presence of a sibling who could offer comfort through touch. When Isma moves to America, she loses this. The absence of Aneeka's hands on her shoulders becomes synonymous with the absence of home itself.

Even in her new environment, Isma attempts to ground herself through physical sensation. Upon arriving at Logan Airport in Massachusetts, she encounters snow for the first time. Rather than simply observing it, she reaches out: "She walked over to a mound of snow at the edge of the parking lot, took off her gloves, and pressed her fingertips down on it. At first it resisted, but then it gave way, and her fingers burrowed into the softer layers beneath" (Shamsie 4). This moment reveals Isma's instinctive need to connect physically with her surroundings. Touch becomes a ritual of acceptance—she tests whether this new place will yield to her, whether she can claim it. Yet the touch is cold, even hostile. The snow "resists" before yielding. Massachusetts does not welcome her with warmth; it merely permits her presence. Unlike Aneeka's soothing hands, the snow offers no comfort—only a transactional encounter.

The most devastating portrayal of touch's absence comes when Isma realizes technology cannot fill the void. After seeing her brother Parvaiz's name appear on Skype—symbolizing his estrangement—Isma reflects on what she has lost: "'Touch' was the one thing modern technology didn't allow, and without it she and her sister had lost something vital to their way of being together" (Shamsie 18). Shamsie strips away the illusion that screens can replicate physical closeness. Isma can see Aneeka's face, hear her voice, but she cannot feel her. Then, alone in her apartment, Isma touches her own shoulder: "She touched her shoulder, muscles knoted beneath the skin. Pressed down, and knew what it was to be without family; no one's hands but your own to minister your own suffering" (Shamsie 18). This self-soothing gesture is heartbreaking—Isma must become her own comforter because no one else is there. The absence of touch is not merely loneliness; it is a fundamental rupture in how Isma defines home.

Through touch, Shamsie argues that home is not a destination but a physical experience. For Isma, home was never London specifically—it was the feeling of her sister's hands on her shoulders, the warmth of bodies in proximity, the security of being physically close to those she loved. When she moves to America, she gains safety and freedom, but she loses the very thing that made her feel at home: tactile connection. Technology promises to bridge the distance, but as Isma discovers, a screen cannot hold you. Home, for Isma, is something she can no longer touch.
