## Detailed Research Findings

### What the Data Says (128 SaaS/Tech Logos Analyzed)

**Structure:**
- 72% use icon + text combined
- 21% use text only
- 6% use icon only
- 30% of logos with an icon incorporate the first letter of the company name
- 33% use basic geometric shapes (square, circle, triangle)
- 67% use graphically complex forms (arrows, waves)

**Typography:**
- 93% use sans-serif font families
- Serif, Serif-Slab, and Script fonts are very rare in tech
- 42% are all-lowercase (e.g., "gusto")
- 48% are title case (e.g., "Gusto")
- 10% are all-uppercase
- 100% of two-word company names have NO space between words in their logos

**Color:**
- 51% are black (no color) — most versatile for B2B
- When colors are used: blue (18%), orange (10%), green (8%) most popular
- Tech companies prefer less color than consumer brands

### What Makes Logos Timeless

From Toptal and Ramotion's timeless branding research:
1. **Anchor to mission and values** — not current trends
2. **Simplicity** — clean, memorable, reproducible at any size
3. **Versatility** — works in full color, black/white, and monochrome
4. **Balance and scale** — foundational shapes and clear symbolism
5. **Avoid trendy details** — centuries-old logos use foundational shapes
6. **Pick colors last** — even without color, the logo must stand alone
7. **Originality** — veer from common symbols while staying relevant
8. **Brand-appropriate** — colors/fonts shape perception and must align with strategy

### What Ages Poorly in Tech Logos

These design choices have NOT aged well:
- Gradient-heavy logos (OpenAI's hexagon being an exception that works)
- Abstract 3D blobs and organic shapes
- Glowing effects and neon
- Overly detailed icons with lots of small elements
- Trendy AI imagery: neural networks, brain outlines, robot faces, glowing orbs
- Thin line work that disappears at small sizes
- Comic/playful fonts for serious B2B brands
- Drop shadows and skeuomorphic effects
- Gradients on text
- Thin stroked geometric patterns

### What Has Aged Well

- Simple geometric marks (FedEx arrow, Apple apple, Slack grid)
- Strong typographic wordmarks with custom lettering
- Single bold color marks
- Hexagonal and circular abstract marks (IBM, OpenAI, Starbucks)
- Marks built from a single meaningful concept
- Clean sans-serif typography at scale

### Borne's Competitive Positioning

Direct competitors: Smith.ai, Vapi, Goodcall, Rosie, My AI Front Desk, CloudTalk, Dialzara, AutomateNexus

Most competitor logos use:
- Abstract geometric marks
- Blue-dominant color schemes
- Robot/AI-related iconography
- Generic tech startup aesthetic

**Opportunity for Borne:** Stand apart with a brand that feels more grounded, reliable, and built-to-last — not another generic AI startup with a glowing orb. Geele's veteran background and security-first positioning are genuine differentiators that should inform the brand identity.

### Logo Types to Avoid for Borne

1. ❌ Robot/AI face icon — everyone in AI does this
2. ❌ Glowing orb/neural network — overused in AI SaaS
3. ❌ Abstract gradient blob — will look dated in 3 years
4. ❌ Speech bubble alone — too generic for communication
5. ❌ Thin line work — won't scale to small sizes (favicon)

### Logo Types That Fit Borne

1. ✅ Shield/security mark — security-first positioning is a differentiator
2. ✅ Compass/direction arrow — autopilot, forward momentum
3. ✅ Structural/geometric mark — engineered, not marketed
4. ✅ Anchor/buttress — "borne" = carried, supported, foundation
5. ✅ Signal/communication wave — voice AI and receptionist function
