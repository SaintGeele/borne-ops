# PART 1: RESEARCH SUMMARY

## What Makes Logos Last (The Data)

**The 128-Logo SaaS Study (Key Facts):**
- 72% of tech/SaaS logos use icon + text combined
- 93% use sans-serif fonts
- 51% are black (no color) — black logos are the most versatile
- When colors are used: blue (18%), orange (10%), green (8%)
- 100% of two-word tech company names have NO space between words in logos
- 30% of logos with icons incorporate the first letter of their name into the icon
- 33% use basic geometric shapes; 67% use complex forms

**Timeless Design Principles (from Toptal, Ramotion, Creative Bloq):**
1. Anchor to mission and values — not current trends
2. Simplicity — clean, memorable, reproducible at any size
3. Versatility — works in full color, black/white, monochrome
4. Balance and scale — foundational shapes and clear symbolism
5. Avoid trendy details — gradients, glowing effects, AI clichés
6. Pick colors last — even without color, the logo must stand alone
7. Originality — veer from common symbols while staying brand-relevant
8. Brand-appropriate — colors/fonts must align with industry and strategy

**What Ages Poorly:**
- Gradient-heavy logos
- Abstract 3D blobs and organic shapes
- Glowing effects, neon, aura effects
- Neural networks, robot faces, glowing orbs (AI clichés)
- Thin line work that disappears at small sizes
- Comic/playful fonts for serious B2B brands
- Drop shadows and skeuomorphic effects

**What Has Aged Well:**
- Simple geometric marks (FedEx arrow, Apple, Slack grid)
- Strong typographic wordmarks
- Single bold color marks
- Hexagonal/circular abstract marks (IBM, OpenAI)
- Marks built from a single meaningful concept
- Clean sans-serif typography at scale

## Borne's Competitive Landscape

Direct competitors (Smith.ai, Vapi, Goodcall, Rosie, My AI Front Desk, CloudTalk) overwhelmingly use:
- Abstract geometric marks
- Blue-dominant color schemes
- Robot/AI-related iconography
- Generic tech startup aesthetic

**Key Opportunity:** Borne's veteran ownership and security-first positioning are genuine differentiators. Most competitors lean into "friendly AI assistant" aesthetics. Borne can lean into "engineered, reliable, built to last" — a more grounded, credible positioning for skeptical small business owners.

# PART 2: THE THREE CONCEPTS

## Concept 1: "The Foundation Mark"
**Core idea:** Borne = carried, supported, foundation

An architectural arch/buttress shape made of clean geometric bars. Negative space creates an upward arrow. The mark literally shows what "borne" means — something that carries, supports, holds up. Combined with the "BORNE SYSTEMS" wordmark in Funnel Display Bold.

**Colors:** Deep Navy `#1B3A5C` + Warm Orange `#E86C2C`

**Why it works:** Most meaningful connection between company name and visual mark. Architectural/geometric communicates engineered-not-marketed. Upward arrow = momentum. Nothing about it looks like an AI startup.

**One-color test:** ✅ Passes — the geometric arch reads clearly without fill.

---

## Concept 2: "The Signal Mark"
**Core idea:** Voice communication + security protection + layered service

A hexagonal or shield-shaped outer container holding three stacked chevron/signal bars. The chevrons represent voice waves (AI receptionist), layered services, and subtly form a "B" letterform. The shield communicates security-first positioning explicitly.

**Colors:** Deep Forest/Olive Green `#3D5A3E` + Slate Blue `#2E4057` + Warm Orange `#D4652B`

**Why it works:** Most distinctive color palette in the AI space (no one major competitor uses green/orange). Shield is the strongest security signal. Stacked chevrons clearly communicate voice/communication without being generic. Best competitor differentiation.

**One-color test:** ✅ Passes — shield silhouette is strong enough without fill.

---

## Concept 3: "The Autopilot Mark"
**Core idea:** Direction + guided motion + autopilot positioning

A geometric "B" wordmark (no curves, all straight lines) with a northward-pointing arrow integrated into the letterform. Three small autopilot dots below "SYSTEMS" as a visual echo of the "YOUR BUSINESS RUNS ON -> AUTOPILOT" tagline. Wordmark-first, with the "B" mark also functioning as a standalone icon.

**Colors:** Deep Navy `#1A3A5C` + Safety Orange `#E05C1A`

**Why it works:** Strongest tagline-to-mark connection. Geometric "B" is ownable — most "B" logos use curves, this one doesn't. Wordmark-first logos (IBM, FedEx, Coca-Cola) are historically the most enduring. Autopilot arrow is universally understood and directly reinforces the core product promise.

**One-color test:** ✅ Passes — geometric "B" with arrow reads clearly in monochrome.

# PART 3: RECOMMENDATION

## My Recommendation: Concept 3 — "The Autopilot Mark"

**This is the strongest logo concept for three compounding reasons:**

**1. Tagline-to-mark authenticity is the hardest thing to create and the most durable.**
"Your BUSINESS RUNS ON -> AUTOPILOT" is not a throwaway tagline — it is the product promise. Concept 3 encodes that promise visually every time someone sees it. The directional arrow integrated into the letter "B" means that every touchpoint of the brand reinforces what Borne does. This kind of authentic mark-to-name connection is the rarest and most valuable thing in logo design.

**2. Wordmark-first logos are the most historically durable brand marks.**
IBM, FedEx, McDonald's, Coca-Cola — wordmarks and letterform marks have outlasted every design trend for a century. The geometric "B" with an arrow inside it is simple enough to survive any resolution (favicon to billboard), distinctive enough to be ownable, and has no AI clichés that will look dated in five years.

**3. It directly serves the target customer — the skeptical small business owner.**
Home services contractors and small service businesses (HVAC, plumbing, electrical) are bombarded with AI startups promising magic. A logo that looks like a precision-engineered system — a bold letterform with an arrow pointing north — communicates reliability and direction before a single word is read. It says "this is built by people who know where they're going."

**The runner-up:** Concept 2 (The Signal Mark) is the better choice if security-first differentiation is the primary brand story. The shield + green/orange palette is the most distinctive in the competitive landscape and directly serves the "security-minded AI" positioning. If Geele wants Borne to be known as "the secure AI choice for businesses who can't afford risk," Concept 2 is the play.

**Concept 1 (The Foundation Mark)** is the most meaningful from a pure linguistics standpoint — "borne" literally means carried — and would be a strong choice if Geele wants to emphasize that his company is the foundation his clients build on. It is the most "quietly confident" of the three.
