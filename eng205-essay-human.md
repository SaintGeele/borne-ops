Sound and the Shattered Self: Identity in Kamila Shamsie's Home Fire

In Kamila Shamsie's *Home Fire*, sound serves as both compass and conqueror—a force that guides characters toward belonging even as it is the violent tearing apart of identity. For Parvaiz, the novel's most tragic figure, sound becomes the terrain upon which he loses himself entirely. What begins as the familiar music of childhood in London gradually gives way to the azan of Raqqa, revealing that home is not a place one inhabits but a sound one answers—a call that, once answered, cannot be unanswered.

The novel opens with sound as the language of intimacy and belonging. On page 86, Parvaiz and his sister Aneeka share "a languorous afternoon, lying on a thick-pile rug, playing favorite songs from their childhood for each other, swapping stories of growing up." This moment establishes music as the sonic texture of their childhood home—a London that, despite its coldness toward Muslims, remains the soundscape of their innocence. The songs are not merely background noise but the very medium through which sibling bonds form and identity takes root. When Aneeka "blow[s] out to make trumpetlike noises in time with the music" against Parvaiz's arm, the image is tender, human, rooted in the particular intimacy of shared childhood sound. Here, sound is welcoming; it wraps the twins in a sonic blanket that says *you belong here*.

Yet the novel quickly disrupts this acoustic belonging. When Parvaiz is recruited by Farooq, the promise that draws him to Karachi is explicitly sonic: he will work on "a popular music show" (153). The lure of heritage comes packaged not as ideology but as pop culture—the familiar sound of Pakistani music industry, the promise that his knowledge of music could make him someone in the land his father died for. This is the first displacement: the sound that should anchor him to London becomes a tool for escaping its disappointments. The music show is a hybrid sound, neither fully Western nor fully Pakistani, that gestures toward a home Parvaiz has never visited but already imagines as his birthright.

The novel's devastating turn comes in Raqqa, where Parvaiz arrives to find a soundscape utterly transformed. On page 158, the city announces itself through competing calls: "at prayer time the azaan would bounce between one slim tower and the next." The word "bounce" is crucial—it suggests not the reverent echo of faith but the frantic, even violent, repetition of a demand. The azan, which for millions of Muslims is the pause and return of sacred time, here becomes a deployment, a call to arms. The palm fronds that "slapped their fronds against one another" and the polyethylene bag "caught in the electric wires" that "made shivering sounds" create a discordant symphony—a city performing its own identity through noise rather than music. Parvaiz has traveled from the songs of childhood to the azan of the caliphate, and Shamsie makes clear that he cannot hear the difference. His ear, which once distinguished favorite childhood songs, now hears the azan as the sound of finally belonging somewhere.

The tragedy of Parvaiz is that he mistakes the call for home. The sound that should unite him with his father instead silences everything that made him who he was—the silly games with Aneeka, the M&Ms from Aunty Naseem, the very particular music of a London childhood. When he realizes his mistake, it is too late; Farooq's bullet finds him before the British consulate can offer another answer to the question of where he belongs.

In the end, *Home Fire* suggests that for the children of diaspora, sound is never neutral. The songs we grow up with, the calls to prayer we learn to answer, the noise of the streets that teach us who belongs and who doesn't—all of it accumulates into a sonic identity that the world insists on parsing, questioning, and ultimately, for Parvaiz, destroying. Shamsie's insight is that you cannot choose your soundscape any more than you can choose your blood—and that some sounds, once let in, will echo long after the door has closed.
